We'd like to share some of Rednal's history, from the helicopter and control tower to the people who worked here and the various duties the site undertook.

1940

RAF Rednal was a Royal Air Force station located near Shrewsbury. It was primarily used during World War II and briefly after the war, but its history is relatively obscure compared to some other RAF stations.

RAF Rednal was constructed in the early stages of World War II, during a time when the British military was rapidly expanding its air defence capabilities in response to the German threat. The airfield was established in 1940 and officially opened in 1941. It was designed as a satellite station for RAF Shawbury, located approximately 7 miles to the east, which was a major training base.

Rednal's primary role was to support training and operational flights for pilots and aircrews. It was used for basic and advanced flying training, with a variety of aircraft types–trainers, bombers, and fighters–stationed there throughout its operational life. Its location was strategic, situated in a relatively rural area but still within reach of critical parts of the UK.

1945

After the end of World War II, RAF Rednal continued to serve the RAF for a short time, but by the late 1940s, the role of smaller, secondary airfields like Rednal was diminishing. The RAF's focus shifted to modern jet aircraft, and the need for extensive training bases in rural areas was reduced. The base was gradually decommissioned, and by the mid-1950s, it had largely ceased operations.

1947

The airfield's control tower was a significant part of its infrastructure. Like many RAF control towers, it was responsible for coordinating flights in and out of the airfield, managing air traffic, and ensuring safety during operations. The control tower was an essential part of the daily functioning of the airfield, particularly during wartime when managing the flow of aircraft, training operations, and potentially combat-related sorties was crucial.

After the closure of RAF Rednal, the airfield and its buildings, including the control tower, were repurposed for civilian use. The site has changed over the years, with parts of it being redeveloped for housing and agriculture.

1950

The Westland Wessex was a British helicopter, originally designed as a naval variant of the American Sikorsky S-58 in the 1950’s, making modifications to suit the requirements of the British military and it served with distinction in both the Royal Air Force (RAF) and the Royal Navy (RN). The Wessex was a versatile and reliable aircraft that served in a variety of roles from the 1960s until the early 1980s, including transport, search and rescue (SAR), and anti-submarine warfare (ASW).

The helicopter played a significant role in several capacities, including:

1. Transport and Logistics: The Wessex was primarily used for troop transport, moving soldiers and equipment into and out of operational areas. Its ability to carry a substantial payload, combined with its ruggedness and ability to operate from forward bases or improvised landing zones, made it an invaluable asset in military logistics.

2. Search and Rescue (SAR): The Wessex was also involved in SAR operations, particularly in challenging weather conditions. It played a key role in maritime SAR missions, including rescuing downed pilots or stranded sailors.

3. Combat Support: The Wessex was also used in various combat support roles, such as troop insertion during conflict zones, including in Malaya, Borneo, and later in conflict zones like the Falklands War (1982). The Wessex was capable of being used in both anti-submarine warfare (ASW) and anti-ship roles, equipped with radar and other sensors.

4. Training: The Wessex was also used for training purposes, particularly for aircrew in both the RAF and Army. Its stability and versatility made it ideal for training missions, including navigation, rescue, and evacuation procedures.

1982

The Westland Wessex was involved in the Falklands War of 1982, where both the Royal Navy and the Royal Air Force used it in various capacities. While they were not used as extensively as some other aircraft, such as the Sea King helicopters, the Wessex still played an important role in the broader logistics effort during the conflict.

By the late 1970s and early 1980s, the Wessex began to be gradually replaced by more modern helicopter models, such as the Sea King and the Chinook. The RAF 468 Wessex helicopter, like other variants of the Wessex, were retired from service in the early 1980s, with the last RAF Wessex retiring from service in 1985. These newer helicopters offered greater range, capacity, and advanced avionics. The Wessex's relatively older design, while still reliable, was no longer suitable for the growing demands of modern military operations.

Despite its retirement from active service, the Wessex helicopter remains a symbol of British military aviation. Its design and capabilities influenced subsequent generations of helicopters, especially in terms of versatility and multi-role operations. The Wessex has remained an important part of military aviation history.

Several Wessex helicopters have been preserved in museums and private collections, where they continue to be an important part of British military aviation heritage. For example, a number of Wessex helicopters are on display at aviation museums such as the RAF Museum Cosford and Fleet Air Arm Museum and here at Rednal Combat we are proud owners of a Wessex 468 on our site for visitors to admire.

2000

Rednal Paintball was a well-known outdoor paintball venue for several decades, thriving in the 2000s. The site, with its former RAF infrastructure and natural terrain, was perfect for immersive paintball experiences, with players engaging in team-based tactical games across fields, wooded areas, and even inside some of the old military buildings, including the iconic RAF Rednal control tower.

2019

In 2019, the site was sold to us, a new ownership group with a vision to expand its offerings. Recognizing the potential of the site not just as a paintball facility, but as a more comprehensive multi activity venue, catering to people interested in varied and immersive combat-themed experiences.

We–Rednal Combat–worked to expand the site’s appeal to different demographics, not just traditional paintball players. We began offering a variety of military simulation games using airsoft equipment, where players can experience mock military operations.

As of the latest we remain a popular destination for combat enthusiasts, family groups, and corporate teams looking for a unique experience. The site is well-regarded for its authenticity, professional equipment, and immersive activities. It's an ideal location for those looking for more than just a standard paintball experience. The venue's terrain—full of wooded areas, old military buildings, and other remnants of its RAF days—continues to provide a distinctive backdrop for all types of games.
